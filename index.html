Alzheimer's Disease in Latin America

Alzheimer's disease and other dementias represent one of the fastest-growing public health challenges in Latin America. The region's demographic transition — characterized by rapid population aging — is accelerating the burden of neurodegenerative diseases at a pace that health systems are unprepared to manage.

📚 State of the Art

Current estimates indicate 4-5 million people living with dementia in Latin America, a figure projected to triple by 2050 due to population aging. Latin America faces unique challenges: lower educational attainment (a known protective factor), high prevalence of vascular risk factors, limited specialist availability, and significant stigma that delays diagnosis. The PAHO estimates that less than 10% of dementia cases in the region receive a formal diagnosis.

📊 Key Findings from Our Data

  • Prevalence: 6-8% in persons 65+ across Latin American countries (2020-2023)
  • Specialist density: 0.5-2.5 neurologists per 100,000 inhabitants (vs. 5-10 in high-income countries)
  • Caregiver burden: 85-90% of care provided by informal (unpaid) family caregivers
  • Economic cost: estimated $15-25 billion USD annually across the region

🏛️ Policy Implications

Evidence from the NCRA Dataset indicates that countries with higher aging index values and lower mental health specialist density face compounded risk. Priority interventions include: expanding primary care dementia detection capacity, training community health workers in early identification, and developing national dementia plans aligned with the WHO Global Action Plan on Dementia 2017-2025.

📖 Further Reading

  • WHO World Alzheimer Report 2021
  • PAHO Dementia: Public Health Priority (2013)
  • Ferri et al. (2005). Global prevalence of dementia. The Lancet.

Mental Health in Latin America

Mental health disorders affect approximately 100 million people in Latin America — about 17% of the population. Despite this burden, the treatment gap (percentage of people not receiving care) exceeds 70% in most countries, driven by shortage of specialists, stigma, and insufficient public funding.

📚 State of the Art

The region has made progress in developing national mental health policies, but implementation gaps remain large. Psychiatric bed density averages 2-3 per 100,000 — far below WHO recommendations of 10-15. The COVID-19 pandemic increased prevalence of depression and anxiety disorders by 25-30% in the region according to WHO estimates.

📊 Key Findings from Our Data

  • Treatment gap: 70-85% across Latin American countries
  • Mental health budget: <2% of total health budget in most countries
  • Psychiatrists: 0.5-3 per 100,000 (vs. WHO recommended 3+)
  • Depression prevalence: 5-8% of adult population

🏛️ Policy Implications

The NCRA mental health specialist dataset reveals stark inequalities between urban and rural areas and between countries. Chile, Argentina, and Uruguay show specialist densities 3-5x higher than Bolivia, Paraguay, and Honduras. This data directly informs PAHO's Mental Health Action Plan implementation at country level.

  • PAHO Mental Health Atlas for the Americas 2022
  • Kessler et al. (2009). Prevalence and treatment gap in mental health disorders.
