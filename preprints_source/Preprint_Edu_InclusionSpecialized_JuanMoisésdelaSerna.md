**Keywords**: Inclusive Education, Neurodiversity, Specialized Education, ADHD, Autism Spectrum Disorder

## 1. Introduction
The global movement towards 'Education for All' (UNESCO) has pushed for the full inclusion of neurodivergent learners in mainstream classrooms. However, the 'one size fits all' mainstream model often fails to provide the specialized sensory and instructional support required for students with Autism or ADHD. This preprint evaluates the outcomes of inclusion vs. specialized settings.

As we navigate these changes, it becomes clear that Inclusive Education is part of a broader ecosystem of educational transformation. For instance, the challenges discussed here are closely linked to other innovations, such as the ones explored in "The Real Impact of Gamification on Cognitive Retention: A Systematic Review of Empirical Studies".

## 2. Method
This study utilizes a systematic review methodology, searching databases like Web of Science, Scopus, and ERIC. We selected approximately 30 core references from the last five years, focusing on meta-analyses and longitudinal studies that provide high levels of evidence.

## 3. Results
Evidence suggests that 'social inclusion' is often successful, but 'academic inclusion' remains a challenge without significant resource investment. Meta-analyses (2021-2024) show that students in specialized settings often make faster academic progress in core areas, while those in mainstream settings develop better social-emotional and adaptive skills. The 'inclusive gap' is most prominent in secondary education.

Current models in this field are often influenced by related paradigm shifts, such as those described in "Competency-Based vs. Content-Centric Education: Realigning Curriculum for the 21st Century Labor Market", where the role of the learner is being redefined.

## 4. Discussion
The binary between 'specialized' and 'mainstream' is becoming obsolete. We advocate for 'Flexible Inclusion', where students have access to specialized resource rooms within mainstream schools. This requires a shift from 'fixing the student' to 'fixing the environment' (Universal Design for Learning). Ethical considerations include the right to belong vs. the right to an effective education. We must ensure that inclusion is not used as a cost-cutting measure for closing specialized schools.

## 5. Conclusions
Successful inclusion requires more than a shared physical space; it requires a radical restructuring of the classroom environment and teacher support systems.

## 6. Future Directions
Future research should focus on the long-term cognitive impacts of these interventions and the development of more equitable access models.
