**Keywords**: Neuroeducation, Neuromyths, Cognitive Neuroscience, Teacher Education, Educational Psychology

## 1. Introduction
Neuroeducation aims to bridge the gap between 'the bench and the blackboard'. However, this translation has often been literal and oversimplified, leading to the proliferation of 'neuromyths'. This paper investigates why myths like 'learning styles' or 'left-brain vs. right-brain' persist despite overwhelming scientific evidence against them.

As we navigate these changes, it becomes clear that Neuroeducation is part of a broader ecosystem of educational transformation. For instance, the challenges discussed here are closely linked to other innovations, such as the ones explored in "Inclusive Education for Neurodivergent Learners: Evaluating the Efficacy of Specialized vs. Mainstream Settings".

## 2. Method
This study utilizes a systematic review methodology, searching databases like Web of Science, Scopus, and ERIC. We selected approximately 30 core references from the last five years, focusing on meta-analyses and longitudinal studies that provide high levels of evidence.

## 3. Results
Surveys (2022-2024) show that over 80% of teachers still believe in learning styles. This persistence is attributed to the 'seductive allure' of neuroscience imagery and the lack of robust neuroscientific literacy in initial teacher education. Our review of intervention studies finds that simply debunking myths is less effective than providing alternative, evidence-based cognitive strategies (e.g., dual coding, retrieval practice).

Current models in this field are often influenced by related paradigm shifts, such as those described in "The Real Impact of Gamification on Cognitive Retention: A Systematic Review of Empirical Studies", where the role of the learner is being redefined.

## 4. Discussion
The danger of neuromyths is not just academic; it leads to the misallocation of limited school resources on expensive, ineffective programs. We must develop a 'critical consumer' mindset in teachers. The discussion emphasizes the role of 'bridge-builders'—interdisciplinary experts who can translate complex neuroscience into usable pedagogical principles without losing scientific nuance.

## 5. Conclusions
Neuroeducation holds great promise, but only if it is grounded in rigorous science rather than catchy metaphors. Teacher training must prioritize cognitive psychology over popular neuromyths.

## 6. Future Directions
Future research should focus on the long-term cognitive impacts of these interventions and the development of more equitable access models.
