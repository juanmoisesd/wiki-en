**Keywords**: Hybrid Learning, Blended Education, Post-Pandemic Pedagogy, Student Engagement, Educational Technology

## 1. Introduction
The forced experimentation with online learning during 2020-2022 has left a permanent mark on education. Hybrid learning, once a niche model, is now a cornerstone of the 'new normal'. This preprint evaluates how the transition from 'emergency remote teaching' to 'intentional hybrid design' has impacted student engagement and academic outcomes.

As we navigate these changes, it becomes clear that Hybrid Learning is part of a broader ecosystem of educational transformation. For instance, the challenges discussed here are closely linked to other innovations, such as the ones explored in "Critical Review of Neuroeducation: Identifying and Addressing Neuromyths in Teacher Training".

## 2. Method
This study utilizes a systematic review methodology, searching databases like Web of Science, Scopus, and ERIC. We selected approximately 30 core references from the last five years, focusing on meta-analyses and longitudinal studies that provide high levels of evidence.

## 3. Results
Studies indicate that students value the flexibility of hybrid models but struggle with the 'transactional distance' of asynchronous components. Engagement is highest in 'HyFlex' models that allow students to choose their mode of attendance weekly. However, a significant 'readiness gap' exists; students with high self-regulation skills thrive, while those lacking these skills perform significantly worse than in traditional settings.

Current models in this field are often influenced by related paradigm shifts, such as those described in "Inclusive Education for Neurodivergent Learners: Evaluating the Efficacy of Specialized vs. Mainstream Settings", where the role of the learner is being redefined.

## 4. Discussion
The success of hybrid learning depends on the 'Social, Teaching, and Cognitive Presence' framework (Community of Inquiry). Simply uploading lectures is not hybrid learning. The discussion highlights the need for 'campus as a social hub' where face-to-face time is reserved for collaborative, high-order thinking. Ethical concerns include the increased burden on faculty to manage multiple modes of delivery and the risk of 'second-class' experiences for remote students.

## 5. Conclusions
Hybrid learning is here to stay, but it requires a redesign of the learning experience rather than just a technological overlay.

## 6. Future Directions
Future research should focus on the long-term cognitive impacts of these interventions and the development of more equitable access models.
